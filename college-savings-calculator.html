What Are the Best College Saving Strategies?

Free 529 Plan Calculator — Find Your Monthly Savings Goal

Yes — starting early and choosing the right college saving strategy can save you tens of thousands of dollars in taxes and interest. The best strategy depends on your child's age, your income, your risk tolerance, and how much you can save each month. But the single most important factor is starting now. Every year you delay, you lose the power of compound growth.

Key Point #1: Compare all available strategies before choosing. The most popular and effective college saving vehicle is the 529 plan. These state-sponsored investment accounts offer tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals for qualified education expenses.

Key Point #2: Roth IRAs offer flexibility for college savings. A Roth IRA allows you to withdraw your contributions (but not earnings) penalty-free for college expenses. This gives you flexibility if your child does not go to college or gets a scholarship.

Key Point #3: Custodial accounts (UTMA/UGMA) have trade-offs. These accounts offer more flexibility than 529 plans but count more heavily against financial aid and have less favorable tax treatment.

Key Point #4: Coverdell ESAs are a niche option. Similar to 529 plans but with lower contribution limits ($2,000 per year) and income restrictions. For most families, a 529 plan is a better choice.

Key Point #5: The front-loading strategy maximizes growth. Some 529 plans allow you to contribute up to five years' worth of gifts at once — up to $90,000 per parent without triggering gift taxes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best college saving strategy?+
The best college saving strategy for most families is a 529 plan. It offers tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals for qualified education expenses. Starting early and contributing consistently is more important than which specific investment you choose.
How much should I save for college each month?+
The answer depends on your child's age, current savings, and the type of college. Use the calculator above to see your personalized number. As a rule of thumb, saving $200-500 per month from birth can cover 50-100% of in-state public college costs.
What is a 529 plan and how does it work?+
A 529 plan is a state-sponsored investment account designed for education savings. Contributions grow tax-free, and withdrawals are tax-free when used for qualified education expenses like tuition, room and board, books, and computers.
Can I use a Roth IRA for college savings?+
Yes, you can withdraw your Roth IRA contributions (but not earnings) penalty-free for college expenses. However, using a Roth IRA for college may reduce your retirement savings, so prioritize retirement first.
How does a custodial account (UTMA/UGMA) work for college?+
Custodial accounts are held in the child's name. They offer flexibility but count more heavily against financial aid (20% of assets counted vs 5.64% for parent-owned 529 plans).
What is the Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA)?+
A Coverdell ESA is similar to a 529 plan but with lower contribution limits ($2,000 per year) and income restrictions. For most families, a 529 plan is a better choice due to higher contribution limits.
How does saving for college affect financial aid?+
Parent-owned 529 plans count as parent assets on the FAFSA, reducing aid by at most 5.64% of the account value. Custodial accounts count as child assets, reducing aid by 20% or more.
What happens to unused 529 plan funds?+
Unused 529 plan funds can be transferred to another family member without penalty. They can also be withdrawn for non-education expenses, but earnings will be subject to income tax and a 10% penalty.
What is the projected cost of college in 18 years?+
Assuming 4% annual tuition inflation, today's $25,000 annual cost will be over $50,000 per year in 18 years — over $200,000 for four years. Use the calculator above to see your specific projections.
Should I prioritize retirement or college savings?+
Prioritize retirement first. Your child can borrow for college, but you cannot borrow for retirement. Aim to save at least 15% of your income for retirement before contributing to a 529 plan.
