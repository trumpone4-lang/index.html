What Are the Best College Saving Strategies?

Free 529 Plan Calculator — Find Your Monthly Savings Goal

Yes — starting early and choosing the right college saving strategy can save you tens of thousands of dollars in taxes and interest. The best strategy depends on your child's age, your income, your risk tolerance, and how much you can save each month. But the single most important factor is starting now. Every year you delay, you lose the power of compound growth.

Let me show you why starting early matters so much. If you start saving $200 per month when your child is born, assuming a 6% annual return, you will have over $80,000 by the time they turn 18. If you wait until they are 10 years old, you will have less than $30,000. That eight-year delay costs you $50,000. The best college saving strategy is simply starting as early as possible.

The most popular and effective college saving vehicle is the 529 plan. Named after Section 529 of the IRS tax code, these state-sponsored investment accounts offer tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals for qualified education expenses. That means every dollar your investments earn never gets taxed — not when you contribute, not when it grows, and not when you withdraw it for college costs. Over 18 years, that tax advantage can add tens of thousands of dollars to your college fund.

But 529 plans are not the only option. Roth IRAs allow you to withdraw your contributions (but not earnings) penalty-free for college expenses. This gives you flexibility if your child does not go to college or gets a scholarship. Custodial accounts (UTMA/UGMA) offer more flexibility but count more heavily against financial aid. Coverdell Education Savings Accounts (ESAs) are another tax-advantaged option, but they have lower contribution limits ($2,000 per year) and income restrictions.

Here is a real-world example. The Johnsons have a newborn daughter. They want to save for her college education at a state university that currently costs $25,000 per year. Assuming 4% annual tuition inflation, that same education will cost over $50,000 per year by the time she enrolls. That is $200,000 for four years. Using the calculator below, they can see exactly how much to save each month to reach that goal.

The calculator shows that saving $300 per month in a 529 plan earning 6% returns would give them about $120,000 by age 18 — enough to cover about 60% of projected costs. To fully fund $200,000, they would need to save about $500 per month. This is the power of the calculator — it takes the guesswork out of college planning.

Another powerful strategy is the front-loading approach. Some 529 plans allow you to contribute up to five years' worth of gifts at once — up to $90,000 per parent ($180,000 per couple) without triggering gift taxes. This gets more money invested earlier, maximizing compound growth. If grandparents want to help, they can also contribute to a 529 plan.

One often-overlooked strategy is the scholarship strategy. Many parents assume they will not qualify for scholarships, but millions of dollars go unclaimed every year. The best way to position yourself for scholarships is to have a high GPA, strong test scores, and unique extracurriculars. Some 529 plans allow you to withdraw the equivalent of scholarship amounts penalty-free, though earnings may be subject to income tax.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best college saving strategy?+
The best college saving strategy for most families is a 529 plan. It offers tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals for qualified education expenses. Starting early and contributing consistently is more important than which specific investment you choose. Use the calculator above to see how much you need to save each month.
How much should I save for college each month?+
The answer depends on your child's age, current savings, and the type of college you are targeting. Use the calculator above to see your personalized number. As a rule of thumb, saving $200-500 per month from birth can cover 50-100% of in-state public college costs.
What is a 529 plan and how does it work?+
A 529 plan is a state-sponsored investment account designed for education savings. Contributions grow tax-free, and withdrawals are tax-free when used for qualified education expenses like tuition, room and board, books, and computers. Many states also offer a state income tax deduction for contributions.
Can I use a Roth IRA for college savings?+
Yes, you can withdraw your Roth IRA contributions (but not earnings) penalty-free for college expenses. This offers flexibility if your child does not go to college or gets a scholarship. However, using a Roth IRA for college may reduce your retirement savings, so prioritize retirement first.
How does a custodial account (UTMA/UGMA) work for college?+
Custodial accounts (UTMA/UGMA) are held in the child's name with an adult custodian. They offer more flexibility than 529 plans but count more heavily against financial aid (20% of assets counted vs 5.64% for parent-owned 529 plans). They also have less favorable tax treatment after the first $2,500 of earnings.
What is the Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA)?+
A Coverdell ESA is similar to a 529 plan but with lower contribution limits ($2,000 per year) and income restrictions. It can be used for K-12 expenses as well as college. For most families, a 529 plan is a better choice due to higher contribution limits and no income phaseouts.
How does saving for college affect financial aid?+
Parent-owned 529 plans count as parent assets on the FAFSA, which reduces financial aid by at most 5.64% of the account value. Custodial accounts count as child assets, reducing aid by 20% or more. Grandparent-owned 529 plans currently do not count as assets but withdrawals count as untaxed income to the student.
What happens to unused 529 plan funds?+
Unused 529 plan funds can be transferred to another family member (siblings, cousins, parents, grandparents) without penalty. They can also be withdrawn for non-education expenses, but earnings will be subject to income tax and a 10% penalty. Some states also recapture state tax deductions.
What is the projected cost of college in 18 years?+
Assuming 4% annual tuition inflation, today's $25,000 annual cost will be over $50,000 per year in 18 years — over $200,000 for four years. Elite private universities currently costing $60,000+ per year could exceed $120,000 per year in 18 years. Use the calculator above to see your specific projections.
Should I prioritize retirement or college savings?+
Prioritize retirement first. Your child can borrow for college, but you cannot borrow for retirement. Aim to save at least 15% of your income for retirement before contributing to a 529 plan. If you have extra room in your budget, then contribute to college savings. The calculator above can help you balance both goals.
